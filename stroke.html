Cerebrovascular Accident (Stroke)

Acute Neurological Deficits & Emergency Nursing Intervention

QUICK NAV:

1. Pathophysiology: Ischemic vs. Hemorrhagic

A stroke occurs when the blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted or reduced, preventing brain tissue from getting oxygen and nutrients.

[Image of the difference between an ischemic stroke and a hemorrhagic stroke in the brain]

Ischemic Stroke (87%)

Mechanism: Obstruction by a thrombus or embolus.

  • Thrombotic: Clot forms at the site (often Atherosclerosis).
  • Embolic: Clot travels from elsewhere (commonly Atrial Fibrillation).
  • Goal: Restore blood flow (Reperfusion).

Hemorrhagic Stroke (13%)

Mechanism: Rupture of a blood vessel leading to bleeding into brain tissue.

  • Causes: Hypertension, Aneurysm, or Arteriovenous Malformation (AVM).
  • Warning: "Worst headache of my life" (Thunderclap headache).
  • Goal: Control ICP and stop bleeding.

2. Emergency Assessment (BE-FAST)

Early recognition is vital. Time is Brain.

B - Balance Sudden loss of balance or coordination.
E - Eyes Sudden vision loss or double vision (Diplopia).
F - Face Facial drooping (ask patient to smile).
A - Arms Arm weakness or drift (ask patient to raise both arms).
S - Speech Slurred speech or difficulty finding words (Aphasia).
T - Time Note the Last Known Well (LKW) time.

3. Acute Nursing Interventions

Golden Hour Rule: CT Scan must be completed and read within 45 minutes of arrival to rule out hemorrhage before giving tPA.
  • NPO Status: Keep patient NPO until a formal Swallow Screen is passed to prevent aspiration.
  • HOB Elevation: Maintain Head of Bed at 30 degrees to reduce Intracranial Pressure (ICP).
  • BP Management: In ischemic stroke, allow "permissive hypertension" (often up to 185/110) to maintain perfusion.
  • Thrombolytic Therapy (tPA): Must be given within 3 to 4.5 hours of Last Known Well time.

Left vs. Right Brain Deficits

Right Brain Stroke: Impulsive, spatial-perceptual deficits, left-sided neglect.

Left Brain Stroke: Aphasia (speech), cautious/slow behavior, right-sided weakness.

NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS)

Standardized 11-item tool to quantify stroke severity.

  • 0 = No stroke symptoms
  • 1–4 = Minor stroke
  • 5–15 = Moderate stroke
  • 21–42 = Severe stroke

Aphasia Types

Expressive (Broca's): Knows what to say but cannot get words out.

Receptive (Wernicke's): Can speak but words do not make sense (Word Salad).
