Asthma: Clinical Management Guide

Chronic Inflammatory Airway Disease & Hyperresponsiveness

QUICK NAV:

1. Pathophysiology: The "Triple Threat"

Asthma is a reversible obstructive disease where the airways overreact to stimuli (triggers). This leads to a three-part obstruction of airflow.

1. Bronchospasm

Smooth muscles around the bronchioles tighten instantly. This is the target for Rescue Inhalers (SABAs).

2. Mucosal Edema

The airway lining swells due to inflammation. This is why Corticosteroids are vital for long-term control.

3. Tenacious Mucus

Thick, sticky mucus plugs the narrowed lumen, further decreasing gas exchange and increasing work of breathing.

2. Advanced Assessment & "Red Flags"

Clinical Indicators

  • Expiratory Wheezing: Musical sounds heard as air is forced out.
  • Cough: Often worse at night or early morning.
  • Retractions: Use of accessory muscles (sternocleidomastoid/intercostal).
  • Chest Tightness: Patient feels like "an elephant is sitting on them."

3. Pharmacology & Home Monitoring

Type Medication Clinical Pearl
SABA (Rescue) Albuterol Use FIRST to open airways. Monitor for tachycardia.
ICS (Controller) Fluticasone / Budesonide Use daily. Rinse mouth to prevent thrush.
LABA (Combined) Salmeterol Long-acting; NEVER used as a rescue inhaler.
Leukotriene Mod. Montelukast (Singulair) Take at night. Blocks the inflammatory response.

4. The Asthma Action Plan

Guided by Peak Expiratory Flow Rate (PEFR) monitoring:

Green (80-100%): Good control. Take daily controller meds.
Yellow (50-80%): Caution. Use rescue SABA, contact provider.
Red (< 50%): Medical Emergency. Take rescue med and go to ER.

Peak Flow Meter

Teaching: Use at the same time every day. Blow out hard and fast. The "Best of Three" readings is recorded.

Asthma Triggers

  • Pollen, Mold, Dust Mites
  • Animal Dander
  • Cold Air / Exercise
  • Strong Perfumes / Smoke
  • Emotional Stress
